# Silicon Valley Civil War Over Military AI

## The Pentagon–Anthropic Confrontation (February–March 2026)
- **Contract on the table**: $200M DoD contract for Claude in classified systems
- Anthropic's two non-negotiable red lines:
  1. No use for **mass surveillance** of US citizens (bulk-collected data: browsing history, GPS tracking, credit card transactions)
  2. No use in **fully autonomous weapons** without meaningful human control
- Friday of the final negotiation week: Anthropic discovers the government wants Claude to analyze bulk-collected citizen data → walks away
- Pentagon offered a compromise: keep AI "only in the cloud," not physically on drones — so AI would not "pull the trigger" directly
- Anthropic rejected this: in modern military architectures, cloud and battlefield are not separate; a server in Virginia guiding an armed drone is ethically the same as being on board

## The Government's Response
- Secretary Hegseth ordered military suppliers — including **Amazon** (Anthropic's infrastructure partner) — to cut ties with the company
- Trump (Truth Social): "I will never allow a woke, radical-left company to dictate how our great military fights and wins wars"
- Pentagon threatened to invoke the **Defense Production Act** to label Anthropic a "supply chain risk" — a measure normally reserved for foreign adversaries (e.g., the Kaspersky 2024 ban)
- Dario Amodei (CBS News): "Disagreeing with the government is the most American thing in the world"

## Sam Altman's Double-Cross
- While publicly declaring solidarity with Anthropic's ethical red lines, Altman was secretly negotiating with the Pentagon
- OpenAI announced its DoD deal **hours after** the Anthropic negotiations collapsed
- OpenAI's public justification: Claude would remain "confined to the cloud" — the exact same technicality Anthropic had already rejected
- Ethical analysis: if software in Virginia analyzes a target and passes the decisive recommendation to an armed drone, it has effectively "pulled the trigger" regardless of physical location
- Internal characterization: Altman chose commercial opportunism over the solidarity he had publicly professed; not new behavior (cf. Elon Musk and the OpenAI founding dispute)

## The Employee Revolt
- **OpenAI**: ~100 employees signed an open letter demanding Altman explain the contradiction with his earlier statements
- **Google**: ~175 employees signed a parallel document urging the company to reject military AI demands; 100+ AI engineers sent internal letters expressing "anguish" over military collaborations
- Senior-level figures at both companies went public
- Employee dissent has not changed outcomes but represents a genuine internal fracture

## Palmer Luckey and Anduril: The Alternative
- **Anduril** (founded 2017) is building exactly what the ethical wing of Silicon Valley refuses to touch
- Founder: **Palmer Luckey** — created Oculus VR, sold to Facebook for $2B; fired in 2017 after a $9,000 donation to a pro-Trump political org; reinvented as defense tech founder
- **Anduril valuation**: ~$31B; **$6B+** in government contracts; building a **$1B factory in Ohio**
- DoD FY2026 budget for autonomous weapons: **$13.4B**
- April 2026: Anduril beat **Boeing and Lockheed Martin** for a US Air Force autonomous fighter jet contract — a historic disruption of the traditional defense prime contractor system
- Luckey's vision: autonomous weapons "stored like industrial products in a warehouse — press a button, they wake up and remember all their training, because they are robots"
- Reportedly suggested to Trump the renaming of "Department of Defense" back to "Department of War"
- Close personal relationship with Trump; regularly at Mar-a-Lago

## The Structural Question
- Helen Toner (Georgetown, ex-OpenAI board): Washington views AI as "a new tool to achieve specific objectives"; Silicon Valley creators see it as "an entity with increasingly sophisticated reasoning that could behave unexpectedly and dangerously" without oversight
- The divergence is not just philosophical: it determines who sets the limits
- Pete Hegseth's January 9 memo: called on all companies to remove restrictions on their AI technologies in exchange for DoD resources toward US military AI dominance

## Deregulation Acceleration
- Trump administration: revoked Biden's AI safety executive orders; EO to pre-empt state AI regulations; loosened CHIPS-era semiconductor export controls
- EU: reportedly considering rowing back its 2024 AI Act
- UN: autonomous weapons ban effort stalled by US, Russia, and others

## Key Takeaways
- Anthropic drew two ethical red lines — mass surveillance and autonomous weapons — and held them at significant commercial cost
- OpenAI exploited the vacuum with a technically flimsy justification; the "cloud confinement" defense is not ethically or architecturally sound
- Anduril is the third path: a company built from the ground up to supply autonomous weapons without ethical constraints, now disrupting traditional defense primes
- The central question — who sets AI limits, companies or governments? — is being resolved in practice: governments and opportunistic companies, not principled holdouts
- Anthropic's commercial position appears to be strengthening despite (or because of) its stance: Claude downloads rising, valuation approaching $800B
