# Anthropic Economic Index: Learning Curves (March 2026)

Source: Anthropic Economic Index Report, published 2026-03-24. Authors: Massenkoff, Lyubich, McCrory, Appel, Heller.

## Overview

- Fifth edition of the Anthropic Economic Index, using a privacy-preserving system to track how Claude is used across the economy
- Sample: 1 million conversations from Claude.ai and 1P API, covering **February 5–12, 2026** (3 months after Opus 4.5, coincident with Opus 4.6 release)
- Two main findings: (1) diversification of use cases, (2) learning curves in AI adoption

## Diversification of Use Cases

- **Coding** remains the most common use (35% of Claude.ai conversations are Computer and Mathematical occupations)
- Top 10 O*NET tasks dropped from **24% to 19%** of Claude.ai traffic (Nov 2025 → Feb 2026) — usage is spreading
- Coding tasks are migrating from Claude.ai to the **1P API** (via Claude Code), where they fragment into many smaller task categories
- 1P API task concentration **increased** (28% → 33%) as coding consolidated there

### Use Case Mix Shifts (Claude.ai)
- **Coursework**: 19% → 12% (winter break effect + new casual users)
- **Personal use**: 35% → 42%
- **Work**: 46% → 45% (stable)
- Rise in personal queries: sports, product comparisons, home maintenance

### Automation vs. Augmentation (Claude.ai)
- **Augmentation** (collaborative, human-in-the-loop): 52% → 53%
- **Automation** (directive, fire-and-forget): 45% → 44%
- Augmentation increased slightly; automation decreased sharply in 1P API

### Average Task Value
- Claude.ai: dropped from $49.3 → **$47.9**/hr (more low-wage personal queries entering the mix)
- 1P API: rose to **$50.7**/hr (higher-value coding consolidating there)
- Both remain well above the US average hourly wage of $37.3

## Key Primitives Shift (Nov 2025 → Feb 2026)

| Metric | Nov 2025 | Feb 2026 | Change |
|--------|----------|----------|--------|
| Human education (yr) | 12.21 | 11.92 | -0.29 |
| AI autonomy (1–5) | 3.38 | 3.41 | +0.02 |
| Human time (min) | 185.53 | 183.77 | -1.76 |
| Human + AI time (min) | 15.35 | 14.30 | -1.05 |
| Human can't do alone (%) | 12.09 | 12.24 | +0.15 |

- Tasks are becoming slightly less complex on Claude.ai as the user base broadens

## Emergent Automation Patterns (API)

- **Business sales & outreach automation**: lead qualification, B2B research, cold-email drafting (share doubled)
- **Automated trading & market ops**: portfolio monitoring, trade proposals, market condition alerts (share doubled)
- Customer Service Representatives: high share of tasks in automated workflows — these jobs may be most exposed to AI-driven transformation

## Geographic Inequality

- Top 20 countries: **48%** of all per-capita usage (up from 45%) — global gap is **widening**
- Within the US: convergence continues but slowing — top 10 states dropped from 40% → 38%
- US states projected to reach roughly equal usage per capita in **5–9 years** (previously estimated 2–5)
- Gini across US states: **0.29** (down from 0.37 in Aug 2025) — converging
- Gini across countries: **0.50** (up from 0.48 in Aug 2025) — diverging

## Learning Curves and Model Selection

### Model Selection Matches Task Value
- Paid Claude.ai users select **Opus** for higher-wage tasks: 55% of Computer and Mathematical tasks use Opus vs. 45% of Educational tasks
- For every +$10 in task hourly wage, Opus share increases by **+1.5 pp** (Claude.ai) and **+2.8 pp** (API)
- API users are ~2x more responsive to task complexity in model selection

### High-Tenure Users Are More Successful
- Defined as users signed up for **6+ months**
- 73.1% task success rate vs. 66.7% for low-tenure users (**+6.4 pp**)
- Use Claude more for **work** (+7.3 pp) and less for **personal** (-4.0 pp) use
- More likely to **iterate** (+3.6 pp task iteration) and less likely to **delegate** (-8.7 pp directive use)
- Use Claude for higher-education-level tasks (+6.6%)
- More diversified usage (top 10 tasks account for 20.7% vs. 22.2%)

### Experience Effects
- Even after controlling for task type, model, language, use case, and country, high-tenure users are **~4 pp more likely to succeed**
- Consistent with **learning-by-doing**: facility with AI improves with experience
- Caveat: could also reflect cohort effects (early adopters are more technical) or survivorship bias

## Labour Market Implications

- Learning curves could **deepen inequality**: early adopters with high-skill tasks get more successful, reinforcing their advantage
- This is a channel for **skill-biased technological change** — innovations raising wages for high-skill workers while depressing them for others
- Early adopters may simultaneously be the most exposed to AI disruption and most aided by AI

## Key Takeaways

- AI use is diversifying away from coding on Claude.ai but concentrating in coding on the API — two parallel adoption paths
- The average economic value of Claude.ai tasks is declining as broader, less-technical users join
- Experienced users are measurably better at using AI: higher success rates, more complex tasks, more collaborative patterns
- Global AI adoption inequality is **widening** between countries even as it narrows within the US
- The learning-by-doing effect suggests early AI investment by workers may compound over time
