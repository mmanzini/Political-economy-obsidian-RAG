# Pakistan Military Rule — A History

Chronological survey of Pakistan's military regimes and "hybrid" arrangements, drawn from Tahir Kamran, *Secret Past, Uncertain Future*, via *Altri Orienti* Ep.167 (Apr 2026).

## Ayub Khan (1958–1969) — The Modernising Dictator

- Took power 27 October 1958 after pushing aside President Iskander Mirza
- Sandhurst-trained, fluent English; presented himself as a moderniser cleaning up "parasite" politicians
- Designed a **"democracy with restricted entry"**: a 1962 constitution with an electoral college of just 80,000 "Basic Democrats" who alone could elect the president and assemblies
- The **"decade of development"**: ~6% growth, US dollars flowing in (JFK welcomed him in 1961 as an anti-communist bulwark)
- **22 industrial families controlled 66% of industry, 79% of insurance, 80% of banking assets** — a paradise for 22 families, an inferno for millions
- Built **Islamabad** next to the army HQ at Rawalpindi: literally fusing state and military
- 1965 Operation Gibraltar: infiltration into Indian Kashmir failed; Indian counter-attack reached Lahore; Soviet-brokered peace forced Ayub to return conquered territory
- The 1965 war shattered the myth of his invincibility; 1968 saw mass student and worker protests; in March 1969 he resigned, illegally handing power to General Yahya Khan instead of the speaker of the National Assembly

## Yahya Khan (1969–1971) — The 1971 Catastrophe

- Held the first true general elections on universal suffrage in December 1970
- Awami League (Mujibur Rahman) swept East Pakistan; Bhutto's PPP won West Pakistan; the two winners did not speak
- The army refused to let power pass to Dhaka
- March 1971: **Operation Searchlight** — massacre of Bengali intellectuals and civilians at Dhaka University; civil war
- India intervened militarily under Indira Gandhi; on 16 December 1971 General Niazi surrendered with 90,000 Pakistani prisoners
- Pakistan lost half its territory and more than half its population; **Bangladesh was born**
- Kamran: not just a military defeat, but the **ideological collapse** of the state's founding premise

## Zulfiqar Ali Bhutto (1971–1977) — Civilian Interlude

- Wealthy Berkeley/Oxford-educated feudal who spoke to the masses as a socialist prophet ("bread, clothing, housing")
- 1973: third and current constitution — federal parliamentary system, approved unanimously; his political masterpiece
- Nationalised banks, industries, schools, insurance — bureaucratised the economy and frightened private investment
- **Launched the nuclear bomb project**, financed by Libya (Gaddafi) and Saudi Arabia
- Authoritarian by temperament despite democratic rhetoric
- 1977 elections: won but with evident fraud; opposition (PNA) coalition mobilised; Bhutto called the army to keep order
- Picked General Zia-ul-Haq — a religious "non-cool" general from a modest background — as Chief of Army Staff, believing him manipulable. **Fatal misjudgement**

## Zia-ul-Haq (1977–1988) — The Islamist Architect

- 5 July 1977: Operation Fair Play — coup; 1973 constitution suspended
- Bhutto rearrested via a reopened murder case; show trial; **hanged 4 April 1979**, buried in secret
- Used **Islam as a political weapon**, not as faith: repressive laws transforming religion into a "social cudgel" against minorities and women
- December 1979: Soviets invaded Afghanistan — Pakistan went from pariah to indispensable frontline state overnight
- Reagan opened the taps: billions in military and economic aid; ISI ran CIA/Saudi funds to Afghan mujahideen
- **Madrasa networks along the Afghan border** — funded by Gulf petrodollars to indoctrinate Afghan and Pakistani refugee youth — produced the **Taliban**
- 17 August 1988: his C-130 *Pak One* crashed on takeoff from Bahawalpur; Zia, most of the army general staff, and US Ambassador Arnold Raphel died; cause never officially established

## The Decade of "Disappointment" (1988–1999)

- Benazir Bhutto (Harvard/Oxford) won 1988 elections — first woman to lead a Muslim-majority country, but real power stayed with army and ISI
- Continuous alternation between Benazir and her arch-rival Nawaz Sharif (industrialist, Zia protégé)
- May 1998: Sharif ordered six nuclear tests in response to India's — Pakistan became a nuclear power, but international sanctions devastated an aid-dependent economy
- 1999: Sharif tried diplomatic opening with India (Vajpayee came by bus to Lahore); the army knifed the peace process via **Operation Kargil** — General Musharraf's secret incursion into Indian-held Kashmir
- October 1999: when Sharif tried to fire Musharraf for insubordination, the army responded with another coup

## Pervez Musharraf (1999–2008) — The 9/11 Beneficiary

- Promised "enlightened moderation"; substance unchanged
- 11 September 2001 saved him: became Bush's key War on Terror ally; billions flowed in
- ISI ran the **double game**: hunting al-Qaeda for Washington while protecting Taliban leadership in Afghanistan as a strategic asset
- Sharif exiled to Saudi Arabia; Benazir to Dubai/London
- Benazir returned via US-brokered amnesty deal; **assassinated 27 December 2007** in Rawalpindi
- Musharraf forced to resign in 2008

## The Brief Democratic Window (2008–2018)

- 2013: for the first time in Pakistan's history, an elected government (Asif Ali Zardari, Benazir's widower) completed a full five-year term and handed power to another elected government (Sharif, third time)

## Imran Khan and the "Hybrid Regime" (2018–2023)

- Kamran reads Khan's 2018 rise not as a spontaneous revolution but as the **installation of a "hybrid regime"** — the army's bet on a third way after tiring of the Bhutto/Sharif dynasties
- ISI manipulated the electoral playing field; the tacit deal: Khan handles economy and public face, the army keeps foreign policy and defence (i.e., the budget and real power)
- Khan eventually tried to act as the real boss — moving toward China, claiming foreign-policy prerogatives
- **April 2022**: removed by parliamentary no-confidence vote — first Pakistani PM removed this way
- Khan refused to retire quietly: organised mass rallies, named generals on social media as plotters
- **9 May 2023**: after Khan's arrest, supporters stormed army installations
- Establishment response: party dismantled, mass arrests, military trials of civilians; Khan jailed on dozens of charges from corruption to disclosure of state secrets

## Kamran's Closing Diagnosis

- Today (2024–2026) the country is back at the starting block: a weak coalition government propped up by the military
- Pakistan's tragedy is not a lack of resources or political talent but **the persistence of a power structure that privileges state security over human welfare**
- Until the civil-military imbalance is resolved, Pakistan remains trapped in its past — checkmated against an uncertain and dangerous future

## Key Takeaways

- Every civilian government in Pakistan's history has been ended by, or has ended in subordination to, the army
- The **Doctrine of Necessity** (introduced 1954) has been the juridical lubricant for every coup
- 1971 Bangladesh secession was an ideological collapse, not just a military defeat
- The **Bhutto-Zia transition** is the template: civilian leader miscalculates the army, picks a "manageable" general, gets hanged
- The Afghan jihad (1979–) created the **madrasa-Taliban-ISI** complex that still shapes regional politics
- The Imran Khan experiment (2018–2022) showed even outsider populists end up consumed by the army that installed them
