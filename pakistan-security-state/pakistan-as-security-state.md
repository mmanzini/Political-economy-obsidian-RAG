# Pakistan as a Security State

Framework drawn from Tahir Kamran, *Secret Past, Uncertain Future* (Bacon House National University, Lahore, 2024), as developed in *Altri Orienti* podcast Ep.167 "Labirinto pakistano" (Simone Pieranni, Chora Media, Apr 2026).

## The Core Thesis

- Pakistan is a **"security state"**: a polity in which border defence and the perception of external threat have conditioned the development of every other institution
- Civilian power has remained permanently subordinated to the military establishment
- The state has manufactured — from birth — a permanent fear of encirclement and siege
- Resources have been diverted almost entirely toward defence, leaving little for education, health, or civil society

## The Original Sin (1947)

- Birth on 14 August 1947 amid the largest mass migration in modern history: ~14 million people crossed the Radcliffe Line; at least 1 million died in sectarian violence
- Pakistan inherited a state apparatus too weak to manage the trauma — no offices, no archives, financial assets blocked by India
- India inherited the institutional continuity of colonial Delhi; Pakistan had to build a government from scratch in Karachi
- The 1947–48 Kashmir war militarised the new nation immediately: the army, not parliament, became the guarantor of territorial integrity

## The Religion Contradiction

- Pakistan was founded on a pan-Islamic ideology: Muslims as a universal category justified a separate state
- Yet this universal religious claim was contained within a national state — an internal contradiction
- The religious foundation gave the state ideological legitimacy but no practical institutions

## The Power Vacuum (1948–1958)

- Founder Muhammad Ali Jinnah (the *Quaid-e-Azam*) died in September 1948, one year after independence
- Successor Liaquat Ali Khan was assassinated in 1951
- The Muslim League — composed largely of *Mohajirs* (migrants from northern India) — never became a national party with a real electoral base
- Six prime ministers between 1951 and 1958: chronic instability
- Real power slid into the hands of two unelected institutions: **the civil bureaucracy** (heir to the Indian Civil Service) and **the army**

## Vice-regalism and the Doctrine of Necessity

- Kamran's term **"vice-regalism"** describes the post-Jinnah governors who acted not as constitutional heads of state but as colonial viceroys
- 1954: bureaucrat Ghulam Muhammad dissolved the Constituent Assembly when it tried to limit his powers — a "constitutional coup"
- The judiciary legitimised the act via the **Doctrine of Necessity** — a juridical precedent later used to justify every subsequent military dictatorship in Pakistan

## Structural Imbalance

- The governing elite was non-elected and drawn almost exclusively from West Pakistan, particularly Punjab
- East Pakistan (today Bangladesh) held the demographic majority but was excluded from power
- Language as detonator: Urdu was declared the sole state language despite being unknown to most East Pakistanis; the 1952 repression of the Bengali language movement (students killed by Dhaka police) opened a wound that never healed

## Why the Army Won

- Cold War alignment with the United States brought military aid that modernised the armed forces and made them hypertrophic relative to a weak civilian state
- The 1958 elections were cancelled by Pakistan's first true coup: the bureaucratic-military establishment feared populist parties (especially Bengali ones) would dismantle their privileges
- President Iskander Mirza imposed martial law and named General Ayub Khan as administrator — then was exiled to London 20 days later by Ayub himself

## Key Takeaways

- Pakistan's "security state" identity is not contingent — it was baked in by the 1947 partition trauma and the immediate Kashmir war
- The army and bureaucracy filled a vacuum left by a politically weak Muslim League and the early deaths of Jinnah and Liaquat Ali Khan
- The Doctrine of Necessity (1954) is the juridical key that has unlocked every subsequent military takeover
- Defence-first resource allocation has structurally suffocated Pakistan's civil society for nearly 80 years
- The contradiction between pan-Islamic universalism and national-state borders has never been resolved
