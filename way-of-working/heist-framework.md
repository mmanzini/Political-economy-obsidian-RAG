# The Heist Framework

A way-of-working model that reframes a software team as a **heist crew**: a small group of specialists pulled together for a defined Job, each playing a Signature role, coordinated through lightweight rituals.

## The Metaphor

A heist works because:

- The crew is **small and chosen for the job**, not a permanent department.
- Every member has a **Signature** — a clear specialty that everyone else relies on.
- The crew runs on a **plan** (the Lookout) but expects it to change mid-job.
- Coordination is **frequent and short** — Check-ins, not status meetings.
- The job ends, the crew disbands or reforms, and lessons are captured in the Rundown.

This is the opposite of a stable team grinding through a backlog.

## Core Concepts

- **Crew.** A small (3–7) group assembled for a specific Job. Crews are temporary by design.
- **Job.** A bounded outcome with a clear definition of done — closer to a heist than to a sprint or a project.
- **Signature.** Each crew member's specialty and the thing the rest of the crew counts on them for. Signatures replace generic role titles.
- **Lookout.** The forward-looking plan: what we expect, what could go wrong, what we'll do about it.
- **Rundown.** The retrospective at the end of the Job: what worked, what didn't, what the next crew should know.
- **Check-in.** Short, frequent coordination — more like a comms ping than a standup.
- **Craft.** The standards and tooling each Signature is expected to maintain and improve over time.

## Why This Shape

The Heist Framework is a response to two pressures:

1. **AI agents collapse the cost of execution**, so the bottleneck is now intent, coordination, and quality — exactly what stable backlog-grinding teams are bad at.
2. **Stable cross-functional teams** accumulate dependencies and political drag; temporary crews avoid this by design.

It pairs naturally with [[from-hierarchy-to-intelligence|From Hierarchy to Intelligence]], which argues for a similar collapse from layered orgs to networks of small autonomous units.

## How It Differs from Agile

- **Sprints → Jobs.** Time-boxed iteration is replaced by outcome-boxed crews.
- **Roles → Signatures.** Generic titles (PM, Dev, Designer) are replaced by specialty + accountability.
- **Standups → Check-ins.** Less ceremonial, more frequent, async-friendly.
- **Retros → Rundowns.** The Rundown is also a handoff document for whoever runs the next related Job.

## Key Takeaways

- Crews are temporary. Don't try to make them permanent — that's how you end up back at departments.
- Signatures are about **what the crew counts on you for**, not what your business card says.
- The Lookout is a living document, not a one-shot plan.
- The framework only works if Jobs are bounded outcomes; vague Jobs collapse the whole model.
