# Jack Dorsey: Every Company Can Now Be a Mini-AGI

Sequoia *Long Strange Trip* podcast (Brian Halligan host, April 2026) with Jack Dorsey (Block CEO) and Roelof Botha (Sequoia, Block board). Primary-source companion to [[from-hierarchy-to-intelligence|From Hierarchy to Intelligence]] — Dorsey personally walks through the rationale, the 40% RIF, and the operating model he is rebuilding Block around.

## The Trigger Event

- **December 2025**: model capability crossed a threshold for *legacy* code bases (not just greenfield prototypes). Hallucination was no longer the dominant failure mode for coding; tool harnesses matured suddenly that month.
- Block leadership went home for the holidays, played with the tools, came back and asked one question around the table: *"Would we build the company this way if we had these tools today?"* Uniform answer: **no**.
- **From decision to execution: under three weeks.** A 40% RIF.

## The Three-Criterion Sizing Exercise

To find the new headcount, Block asked three questions in order:

1. Minimum people to keep the service up 100%.
2. Minimum people to stay fully in compliance with regulators (Block is heavily regulated).
3. Minimum people to grow, fulfil Street commitments, **and** rebuild the company as an intelligence.

Plus a buffer for mistakes. That set the number.

## The Three Roles

Block is normalising to three roles only:

1. **IC** — builder/operator (engineer, designer, salesperson, PM). Augmented by agents so one IC can do what a team of ten used to. Durable human skill: **judgment, taste, creativity**.
2. **DRI** — owns customer outcomes. Sets strategy, assembles ICs. Durable human skill: **ownership and accountability**.
3. **Player-Coach** — replaces traditional managers. Builds capability in others *by doing the work alongside them*, not by directing. Critically, in the future this is an **assignment, not a reporting line**. Durable human skill: **building human capacity, coaching**.

One person can hold all three; Dorsey expects his leadership team to.

## Org Shape

- Current max depth Dorsey → any employee: **5**. Goal for this year: **2–3**. Ideal: **0** — all 6,000 report to him, mediated by the intelligence layer.
- Visualisation: not a pyramid, not a flat org — a **circle** with the company's intelligence at the centre and humans on the edge constantly aligning it toward customer outcomes. Halligan dubs this **"Dorsey mode"** (vs. manager mode and founder mode).

## The Four Layers Of The Company

Dorsey's mental model of what a company actually *is*, post-rebuild:

1. **Capabilities** — the underlying tools (issue cards, accept cards, P2P, lending…).
2. **Interfaces** — Square, Cash App, Tidal, Bitkey, Proto. Where capabilities touch humans.
3. **Proactive intelligence** — using customer signal to *prompt the customer* instead of waiting to be prompted (e.g. cash-flow protection: never go to zero).
4. **World model** — deep, evolving understanding of the company *and* the customer.

## The Roadmap Becomes The Customer

> "Our limiting factor as a company is our road map. We need to remove that from the equation."

The aspiration: a customer asks for a feature that doesn't exist; the system **composes it in real time** from existing capabilities. When it can't, that gap **becomes the next thing to build**. Customer queries replace customer research, interviews, support tickets, and product feedback as the source of roadmap truth — the same insight as [[from-hierarchy-to-intelligence|From Hierarchy to Intelligence]] but stated more bluntly.

## "Money Is The Most Honest Signal In The World"

Same line as the Sequoia article. Dorsey reinforces it: transactions don't lie, and that data fidelity is what makes Block's customer world model load-bearing. He concedes the model only works if your business "understands something of human nature deeply and gets deeper every time" — otherwise AI is just an add-on and you'll rhyme too closely with the frontier labs to differentiate.

## What's Different In Day-To-Day Operations

- **Meetings**: two months ago, slide decks and Google Docs. Now **everyone brings a working prototype** (real or simulated data) they can modify in real time. Conversations happen *around* the artifact, not the abstraction.
- **Cost of being wrong on a path** is approaching zero, so exploration breadth has gone up. Dorsey now encourages parallel exploration; focus comes in on the *last 20%* where taste and judgment matter.
- The 80/20: tools deliver ~80%; the remaining 20% — creativity, taste, judgment — is the human's job.

## CEO Job Redefinition

Past: (1) right principles/team/culture, (2) ensure decisions reflect customer + market, (3) raise the execution bar.

Future: those still hold, but mostly **architecting the company as an intelligence** and being "the extra checkpoint on whether the alignment circle of humans on the edge is working correctly."

## Roelof Botha's Frame

Echoes Adam Smith's *Wealth of Nations*: with the right signals, self-interested participants converge on optimal outcomes. Hierarchies introduce political distortion; an intelligence layer with ground truth removes it. Pushes back gently on Halligan's framing: **the AI does not make most of the decisions** — it communicates alignment; humans set the objective function (growth rate, gross profit per employee, NPS — some combination); customers, via their queries, effectively make most product decisions.

## CEO Advice (Second Half Of The Episode)

- **Boards**: "Treat your first board as a hire you can never fire — and they can fire you." Optimise for the *person*, not the brand. Add an independent within a year or two of PMF; never assemble a board in a rush before IPO.
- **Two CEO roles at once**: fine for private companies, never for public. Should be an anti-goal.
- **Biggest career regret**: delegating too much. Treating Block as a holding company with separate Square/Cash App CEOs created differing cultures and execution levels. "A mess." A legible company changes the equation.
- **Second act**: don't think of it as a second act. Get comfortable losing credibility with stakeholders; you can earn it back if the principle is right. Cash App was hated internally for 2–3 years and mentioned <8 times in the S-1. Now it's >50% of revenue.
- **DGAF as a quality**: it wavers; reframe every encounter as a mentor. "My only regrets are where I decided not to learn something."
- **Meditation**: not woo-woo. A physical practice for sharpening focus and observation, and decoupling stimulus from reaction.
- **Timeless CEO qualities (Botha's ALE)**: Authenticity, Logic, Empathy.
- **New CEO quality (Dorsey)**: ability to *reprogram your own assumptions* constantly — and to treat AI output as **input**, not output. The default trap is offloading judgment to the intelligence layer.

## Key Takeaways

- The Dorsey thesis is a **structural** claim, not a productivity claim — "I don't think this is a productivity thing, I think it's a structural thing that needs to shift." Anyone framing AI as 10x copilots is missing the point.
- The decision velocity matters as much as the decision: three weeks from question to 40% RIF, *deliberately ahead* of the moment when public-company pressure would force it. The integrity of the move depends on not being cornered.
- Player-coach as **assignment, not reporting line** is the most operationally radical idea here — it severs the link between coaching and authority that every manager career ladder is built on.
- The "roadmap = customer queries" reframe is the concrete mechanism that the original Sequoia article gestured at but didn't fully spell out.
- Botha's correction matters: in this model AI is the **alignment communication layer**, not the decision-maker. Customers (via queries) make product decisions; humans on the edge align the system.
- The model only works if your business has a deep, deepening understanding of some part of human nature *and* a high-fidelity signal (Block has money). For businesses without that signal, this is an add-on, not a rebuild.
