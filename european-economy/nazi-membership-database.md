# Nazi Membership Database: Memory Politics in Germany and Austria

## The Database
- **Source**: US National Archives; digitised and made public in **March 2026**
- **Contents**: Records of millions of Germans who joined the NSDAP (Nazi party) in the 1930s–40s
- **Scope**: Estimated **~8.5 million NSDAP members** — roughly **1 in 5 adults** of the era
- **Access before**: Possible but "macchinoginoso" (cumbersome) — required navigating raw archival data

## Die Zeit's Contribution
- German newspaper *Die Zeit* organised the data into a **searchable database** accessible to the public
- Home page tagline: "Approfondite il passato nel partito nazista della vostra famiglia" ("Explore your family's Nazi past")
- Result: **millions of visits** within hours of launch; thousands of public comments

## Public Reaction
- Many users found records of close family members, despite family narratives of non-involvement
- Sample comment (person, 71 years old): "I've already found the names of two close relatives, despite the family always saying no one was involved. Changing my perspective at 71 is a bitter shock."
- The database broke open a taboo that persisted in German and Austrian families since the postwar decades

## Memory Politics Dimension
- The taboo around Nazi family history is a documented postwar phenomenon; generations were raised with silence or denial
- Public database tools accelerate individual confrontation with collective historical guilt in a way that state-imposed historical education could not
- Austria parallel: Austria also digitising and opening Holocaust-era records; separately building a **police station inside Hitler's birthplace** (Braunau am Inn) to deter neo-Nazi pilgrimages

## Key Takeaways
- ~8.5 million Germans were NSDAP members — a far higher number than the postwar "we didn't know / no one was involved" narrative admits
- The Die Zeit database made this searchable for the first time at scale, triggering widespread family discoveries
- The public response — millions of visits, thousands of comments, shocked testimony — demonstrates latent demand for individual reckoning with inherited history
- Austria's choice to turn Hitler's birthplace into a functioning police station is a policy-level complement: physical erasure of a pilgrimage site
