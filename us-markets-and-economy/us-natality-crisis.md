# US Natality Crisis

## Current Data (2025)
- 2025: **worst year for US births in 25 years**
- Births fell **1%** from 2024; fertility rate (births per 1,000 women of reproductive age) also declined
- Natural increase (births minus deaths): just over **500,000** — historically low
- Milestone: for the first time, **mothers over 35 outnumbered mothers under 25**

## Long-Term Trend
- Birth rate declining since the **2007–08 Great Recession** — the original driver was economic instability
- The economy recovered strongly in subsequent years (high wages, low unemployment in 2010s) — birth rate **did not recover**
- Experts uncertain on root cause; surveys show the percentage of Americans wanting children has remained stable for decades
- Suggests the decline is structural, not a response to economic conditions

## Structural Drivers
- **Women's reproductive autonomy**: greater control over timing → waiting for the right partner and the right moment; fewer unwanted pregnancies
- **Teen pregnancies**: down **7%** vs 2024; down **72%** since 2007 — the dominant contributor to the overall trend
- **Age at first birth**: 2025 milestone — mothers over 35 outnumber those under 25 for the first time
- **Cost of living**: rising housing and childcare costs make two incomes standard; some couples choose child-free (DINK) lifestyles
- **IVF access gap**: fertility treatment costs up to **$20,000 per cycle**; fewer than **25%** of infertile couples who want children have access

## Trump Administration Response
- **Rhetoric**: pro-natalist emphasis on family and large families; praise for those with many children
- **NatalCon** (annual gathering): mix of religious conservatives and Musk-influenced "have as many children as possible" advocates; auction-style atmosphere ("Who has more than 7 children? 9? Who beats 9?")
- **IVF expansion attempt**: early announcement; a senior health official predicted a boom of "Trump babies"
- **Result**: no effective policy mechanism followed; 25 states have mandated some insurance fertility coverage but with restrictive requirements that limit practical access
- **Alabama precedent (2024)**: state Supreme Court ruled frozen embryos have legal personhood; IVF clinics suspended operations; state legislature subsequently passed a law to neutralize the ruling — but the legal risk remains and has chilled the sector in several Republican-led states
- **Minnesota**: working on fertility coverage legislation

## Key Takeaways
- US birth rates at 25-year lows despite economic recovery, confirming the trend is structural not cyclical
- The shift to older first-time mothers and dramatically lower teen pregnancies reflects long-term social change that is largely irreversible via policy
- IVF access is deeply unequal: cost, insurance patchwork, and anti-abortion legal risks all compound the barrier
- Trump's pro-natalist rhetoric has produced no effective policy; the religious conservative wing — which views declining births as moral collapse rather than demographic problem — complicates any pragmatic fertility-support approach
- Demographic trajectory: without policy change or immigration, the US natural increase will continue to narrow
