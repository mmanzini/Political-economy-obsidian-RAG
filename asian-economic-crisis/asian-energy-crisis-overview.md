# Asian Energy Crisis Overview

The Hormuz blockade has hit Asia disproportionately: 80% of oil and 90% of LNG transiting the strait is destined for Asian markets. The crisis is described as asymmetric — the war is fought in the Mediterranean with American and Israeli weapons, but Asia pays the steepest economic price.

## Chain Reaction

1. Energy scarcity → prices soar
2. Inflation erodes savings (transport, food cost double)
3. Governments burn budgets on subsidies to cap prices
4. Social stability cracks — protests emerging

## Key Data

- Global petroleum supply lost ~11M barrels/day
- Philippines, Vietnam, Indonesia: oil reserves for only ~3 weeks of normal demand
- Half of global flight cancellations last week involved Asian airport routes
- If war continues, Southeast Asian growth could drop 2–3 percentage points (Legend estimate)

## Environmental Reversal

- Japan authorised return to maximum coal plant capacity, revoking emission limits
- India (Gujarat) ordered reopening of coal plants
- Philippines authorised Euro 2 / high-sulphur fuels despite environmental damage
- Vietnam announced nuclear plant construction (with Russia)
- Green policies shelved across the continent

## Historical Pattern

- IMF data: near-mathematical link between fuel price increases and protests
- In 2022, 25% of South Asian protests were linked to food and energy
- Risk of "protest shock" similar to Sri Lanka government collapse in 2022
- Analysts note a recurring pattern: governments patch emergencies with subsidies instead of addressing structural maritime supply chain vulnerability

## Key Takeaways

- Asia faces a physical scarcity crisis, not just a price shock
- The impact is asymmetric: US is resilient (net exporter); Asia bears the brunt
- Green transition is being paused or reversed as countries scramble for any energy source
- Risk of the energy crisis cascading into social unrest, currency crises, and recession
