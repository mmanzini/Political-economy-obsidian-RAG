# Predatory World Order: Machiavelli vs Sun Tzu

## The Venezuela Blitz (3 January 2026)
- US special operation: **Nicolás Maduro and his wife arrested** in Caracas; ~80 casualties (US sources)
- Trump: Venezuelan oil "will be ours" — to be sold to Russians and Chinese for dollars
- Ends decades of Western half-measures: sanctions, diplomatic isolation, tepid opposition support
- All prior approaches left Maduro in power; the blitz removed him in one night

## The Doctrine of the Predatory Order
- **"Men must either be caressed or annihilated"** (Machiavelli, *The Prince*, 1513)
- Half-measures are dangerous: a lightly wounded enemy has time to regroup, and may one day retaliate; a definitively removed enemy cannot
- Trump applied this logic — the end of partial-measures diplomacy
- The same structure applies to the Iran conflict: escalation from sanctions to military action follows the predatory doctrine
- International law: now openly set aside when inconvenient — not the first time (cf. Kosovo), but the first time it is explicitly not pretended to matter

## Trump as Machiavellian Prince: Fox + Lion
- Machiavelli: the ruler must combine the cunning of the **fox** (simulate, dissemble, wait) with the force of the **lion** (decisive strike)
- Venezuela execution:
  - **Fox phase**: simulated disinterest; allowed Chinese diplomacy to proceed; let Maduro feel secure
  - **Lion phase**: overnight operation, definitive result
- World response: shock, fear, silence — no country moved to defend Caracas
- Validates Machiavelli's core claim: **fear is more durable than love** — love is broken by self-interest at the first opportunity; fear is sustained by the memory of punishment
- Key constraint: *"It is enough not to be hated"* — don't touch citizens' property or disrupt commerce; then fear and non-hatred can coexist

## Xi Jinping as Sun Tzu: Strategic Patience
- China's reaction to Venezuela: apparent silence; internal nationalist pressure to react
- Sun Tzu, *The Art of War* (~500 BCE): *"The expert in battle does not move — he makes sure the enemy comes to him"*
- Xi's calculus: Trump wants China to react and be drawn into open conflict where US military superiority is still overwhelming; Xi refuses the bait
- Goal: let US "victories" become costly quagmires; wait for imperial overextension
- *"There has never been a prolonged war from which a country has benefited"* (Sun Tzu)
- Trump = fire that consumes quickly; Xi = water that shapes through adaptation and time

## The Risk of Chinese Paralysis
- Xi has eliminated internal dissent by centralizing power — but may have also eliminated strategic flexibility
- Nationalist base demands visible strength; prolonged immobility is read domestically as weakness
- Mencian risk: if the population loses faith in the ruler's ability to deliver national rejuvenation, regime legitimacy erodes
- Sun Tzu on internal risk: *"There are 5 circumstances in which victory can be predicted; one is when the general is not constrained by functionaries of the court"* — Xi is now both general and court functionary

## Mencio's Third Way: Legitimacy of Power
- Machiavelli teaches how to hold power; Sun Tzu how to win without fighting; **Mencio** (~4th century BCE) asks what makes power legitimate at all
- A king, fearing for his own head, asks Mencio: was it permissible for a subject to kill the tyrant Jie?
- Mencio: *"He who violates benevolence I call a bandit. He who violates justice I call a criminal. I have heard that a mere individual named Jie was executed. I have not heard that a ruler was killed."*
- Implication: a ruler who abandons virtue loses the right to the title — and can be legitimately removed
- Offers a moral framework beyond the cynical realism of both texts
- The question for our era: at what point does the predatory order forfeit its own legitimacy?

## Key Takeaways
- Trump has operationalized Machiavellian predatory doctrine: half-measures abolished, decisive action normalized, international law discarded when inconvenient
- The world has responded with fear rather than organized resistance — this validates the strategy in the short term
- Xi Jinping is playing a Sun Tzu long game: avoid direct confrontation, let US overextension accumulate, wait
- Both strategies carry internal risks: Trump's impulsiveness risks quagmires; Xi's patience risks being perceived as paralysis by nationalists
- Mencio's challenge: the long-run question is not strategic but moral — rulers who forfeit virtue eventually lose the mandate
