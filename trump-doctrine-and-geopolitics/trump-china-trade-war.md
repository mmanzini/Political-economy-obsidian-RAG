# Trump-China Trade War and Tariff Strategy (2025–2026)

*Sources: PIIE, CNBC, Yale Insights, CS Monitor — compiled April 2026.*

## Legal Architecture

- **April 2025**: Trump raised tariffs on China by 145 percentage points under IEEPA
- **SCOTUS ruling (2026)**: President cannot use IEEPA to impose tariffs — forced legal recalibration
- New framework: global 10% tariff under **Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974**, valid for 150 days (expires ~July 24, 2026)
- **March 2026**: new Section 301 investigations launched against China, Vietnam, Taiwan, Mexico, Japan, EU, and others

## Current Tariff Levels (as of April 2026)

- **30% total on Chinese goods**: 20% "fentanyl" tariff + 10% "reciprocal" tariff
- **October 2025**: additional 100% tariff imposed as retaliation for China's rare earth export controls
- Pattern: **escalation-to-deal cycle** — each tariff round used as negotiating leverage, not as a stable policy endpoint

## Beijing Summit (May 14–15, 2026)

- Trump's first visit to China in eight years — the "fox" coming to the "lion's" den
- Key agenda: tariffs, Taiwan arms sales, tech transfer, critical minerals
- Trump's leverage claim: $40B in US agricultural exports to China
- **Outcome assessment (Yale Insights)**: summit revealed limits of tariff-war strategy — Xi refused to fully capitulate; deals fragile and short-horizon
- **Beijing framing**: Trump coming to Beijing, not the reverse, is read as validation of Sun Tzu patience — *"the expert in battle does not move; he makes sure the enemy comes to him"*

## Xi's Counter-Strategy

- China imposed export controls on **rare earth minerals** as retaliation for US tariff pressure
- Rare earths = chokepoint for US defence industry and clean energy supply chains
- Strategic patience operationalised: hold out, let US legal system constrain Trump (SCOTUS ruling), then negotiate from position of strength
- Xi read the forced legal reframing as evidence of overextension
- Trump's pivot to Section 122 confirms Sun Tzu's observation: a prolonged tariff war benefits neither side — Xi waited for US to blink first

## Structural Limits of the Predatory Doctrine Against China

- The [[predatory-world-order]] doctrine (annihilate or caress; half-measures abolished) functions well against weak-state targets (Venezuela, Iran)
- China is a **peer competitor** — large enough economy to absorb shocks, retaliate asymmetrically (rare earths), and outlast political cycles
- The tariff war exposed: economic coercion has diminishing returns above a threshold of target-economy scale
- Trump's fox-lion strategy requires a sufficiently overawed opponent — Xi is neither awed nor overextended

## Rare Earths as Strategic Chokepoint

- China controls ~60% of global rare earth mining and ~85% of processing capacity
- Export controls on rare earths directly threaten US defence supply chains (F-35, guided munitions, EV batteries)
- Trump's AI chip export controls (see [[cybersecurity-policy-2026]]) mirror China's rare earth move: both parties weaponising supply chain chokepoints
- The emerging pattern: a bilateral decoupling race where each side seizes the chokepoints it controls

## Key Takeaways

- Trump's tariff escalation (145 percentage points under IEEPA) was partially struck down by SCOTUS — a structural constraint on the predatory doctrine when applied to economic policy
- The escalation-to-deal cycle has produced 30% tariffs on Chinese goods as of April 2026, but no stable framework — deals are fragile and short-horizon
- Xi's counter-strategy (rare earth export controls + strategic patience) has successfully constrained Trump's leverage and forced him to come to Beijing
- The predatory doctrine's core weakness: it works decisively against targets that cannot absorb punishment; China can
- The Beijing summit signals not a resolution but a temporary de-escalation in a structural competition that will persist beyond both leaders
