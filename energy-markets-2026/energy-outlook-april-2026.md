# Energy Markets: Mid-April 2026

## The Paper–Physical Oil Divergence
- **Brent futures**: ~**$95/barrel** — stabilized on de-escalation hopes
- **Physical Brent**: above **$130/barrel** — actual shortage conditions unresolved
- This is the defining market anomaly: financial instruments anticipate resolution; real-world supply remains cut off
- Analyst Birolli: "We are short 13 million barrels/day; 8 infrastructure assets are damaged; repairs will take 2–3 years; futures prices do not reflect the severity of the crisis"

## Hormuz: Still Effectively Blocked
- CENTCOM data: no vessels have transited the strait freely
- US intercepted 9 Iranian-linked ships; 6 were turned back to Iranian ports — including a sanctioned Chinese vessel (Riccistarre)
- China's import alternatives: 11% from UAE, 14% from Saudi Arabia, 20% from Russia — more expensive but available
- **April 16**: Iran evaluating a pause in vessel blocking as a de-escalation gesture
- **US Iranian oil waiver expires April 19**: this waiver had permitted ~140M barrels of Iranian crude to reach global markets since the start of the conflict; expiry will tighten supply further even if talks are proceeding

## IEA Data: First Demand Destruction Since COVID
- IEA forecasts a **decline in global oil demand** in 2026 — first time since COVID
- Current demand reduction: ~**80,000 barrels/day** already in effect
- Mechanism: prices are high enough to suppress consumption — a normal market signal preceding structural adjustment
- "Price signals matter; cutting fuel excise taxes (as Ireland attempted) merely sustains demand in a supply-constrained environment and does not address the root cause"

## Russia's Windfall
- Russia's oil revenues **doubled in March 2026**: rose to **$19B** from $9–17B in February
- The Hormuz closure inadvertently benefited Russia: prices spiked; demand redirected toward Russian oil
- Russia is therefore financially incentivized to see the crisis persist

## Natural Gas
- **TTF** (European benchmark): returned to 7-week lows at ~**€43.175/MWh** — approaching late-January pre-war levels
- Some divergence from oil: gas has found alternative routing and LNG supply has stabilized somewhat

## Other Commodities and Cross-Assets
- **Dollar**: stable at $98.16; **EUR/USD**: 1.17 (approaching pre-war levels)
- **Gold**: ~$4,849 (stable)
- **Silver**: nearing $80
- **Bitcoin**: ~$74,300; Goldman Sachs filed with the SEC for the first Bitcoin ETF
- US 2-year Treasury yield: stable at ~4.5%

## Long-Term Implications
- IMF: if the strait remains closed, expect "an unprecedented boom in solar, wind, and nuclear" — sectors to position in
- CoreWeave/Bloom Energy deal: 3GW of power secured for AI data centers through 2027; Bloom market cap surged $70B
- The energy crisis is accelerating capital allocation toward energy security and non-fossil diversification — structural tailwind regardless of conflict outcome

## Key Takeaways
- The paper/physical oil split means financial markets are optimistic while the real supply shock is unresolved
- Russia's doubled oil revenues are a direct financial benefit from the crisis — structurally misaligns Russian incentives with conflict resolution
- The April 19 waiver expiry will tighten actual supply further, even if diplomatic progress continues
- IEA demand destruction (first since COVID) signals the price has already risen enough to suppress real consumption
- Long-term: the crisis is a forcing function for accelerated energy transition investment regardless of near-term resolution
